Towards a study of information geographies: (im)mutable augmentations and mapping of the geographies of information by Graham et al investigates the inequality of access, contribution, and representation amongst the internet. The reading does this by explaining visual representation with the use of maps to indicate the numbers of internet access, contribution, and representation on the internet. In this instance, the map itself stays the say but the alterations are done on the map are ever-changing. This form of a display according to the writing is an example of (im)mutable augmentations which is defined as something that does not change but with changing situations.

Introduction:

According to Graham et al information has always had geography it comes from someplace and evolves someplace, and information is widely influenced by the place that is created. And therefore, the politics and power dynamics are dependent on and influenced by the information created in that place. Although the information is mobile, once it is moved around it is bound to change due to its change of location to suit the place that it is now currently in. As information moves around and is exposed to new places it is bound to evolve and change. “It is precisely this mobility and adaptability of information that provides the motivation to constrain the mutability of information through the creation of what Latour (1986) refers to as ‘immutable mobiles’: or information that can be transported without significant change to its inherent characteristics or meaning.” (Graham et al, 2015 p89) According to Graham et al immutable mobiles are physical indicators of information such as a book that contains information that can’t be altered through distribution, the information encoded onto the object maintains its influence from where it originated with little to no changes after being distributed. ICTs have established the development of information through (im)mutable augmentations “characterized by the layering of dynamic information across and over geographic space. Thus, not only does information have geographies, but geography itself is layered, defined, and augmented by information that is more or less immutable”(Graham et al, 2015 p89) Graham et al argues that (im)mutable augmentations of information about places are important because they influence how we can find and understand different parts of the world. Because if a location such as a restaurant is omitted from a map it can simply cease to exist because people can’t find it and you cannot know or go-to place that you don’t even know exists and aren’t sure about its location either. Geographic augmentations are therefore much more than just representations of places but form as part of the place and become a reflection of it and shape it as well. Information is power according to the article “the ‘geography of information’ – the geographic distribution of information either as a phenomenon in its own right or as representative of some other underlying process (Wilson 2015) – has long been a key means of control and power formation.” (Graham et al, 2015 p89)

Digital pieces of Information:

Before the takeover of the digital age, information was limited and the creation, access, and distribution of information were held by a small group of people. Access to information was controlled by those who displayed it and can understand it. It was mainly only available to those who had a specific skillset in how to store, create, organize and catalogue information at a large scale. And due to how the world worked at the time information was commonly controlled by the global North therefore creating a lack of access, creation, and storage of information in the South.

Changing geographies after the Information revolution:

The rise of the digital age however has allowed many people across the globe to have the freedom to access, create, store, and distribute information. Although just because this is an option it is, however, not available to everyone due to geographical circumstances. This article aims to investigate how the global populace alters how information is shared, accessed, and created.

Through these three topics:

1) Geographies of Access and Enablement

2) Geographies of Participation

3) Geographies of Representation

Geographies of Access and Enablement:

This topic refers to the number of people per country that have access to the internet and is represented through an augmented map. The size of each country represented is determined by its population of internet users. The shading of each country represents its internet penetration rate the dark shades indicate a higher penetration rate than those shaded light. Overall, the map demonstrates a high use of internet users in the North compared to the South. The article has also noted that the lack of internet access in the South is due to income and the high prices placed on internet access compared to that of the North, therefore, hindering access.

Geographies of Participation:

Another vital aspect of accessing digital information is participation. The article measures internet participation through firstly domain names which shows that a large portion of domain names is distributed in the North. Which has been classified that the reason might be because in the South some countries have strict restrictions on registering domain names. Secondly, the article uses Guthub as this is a very large platform on which internet users can publish, contribute, and edit work. And this relates to collaborative coding and again the article shows that the North had more Github commits than the South. The third aspect is Wikipedia contributions, as Wikipedia is widely known to allow people to be able to edit and contribute to the site. But information suggests that Africa has one of the lowest contributions to Wikipedia.

Geographies of Representation:

This topic aims to show places that are represented and underrepresented in the digital world. The article makes use of data taken from Google search data which yielded similar results to those discussed above. That the North yields more results than the South.

Conclusion:

The article in conclusion has brought to my attention that not all places have the same access to the internet due to geographical circumstances. But the internet is excluding certain parts of the world and not making it easy for them to gain access. But the way the internet is set up isn’t entirely the reason to blame but the way things have been generated from the start where the North has always had the power and still maintains it. The South is trying to catch up but circumstances in those places don’t make it easy as well as the power dynamics and dictatorship of some countries where sometimes the internet is prohibited or prices to access the internet are high and form an unnecessary expense to some people who cannot afford it.
